## The Moment That Makes This Possible

In 2012, Foursquare built check-ins. 55 million users showed up. Then they didn't.

Not because check-ins were wrong. Because the cultural moment was wrong.

Instagram launched. Snapchat exploded. Twitter scaled. Checking into places felt mundane next to posting a perfect photo. The era rewarded performance. Social media won.

That era is ending.

Gen Z grew up as the most digitally connected generation in history — and developed the most profound disillusionment with digital life. They don't want more feeds. They don't want to perform. They're exhausted by the gap between who they appear to be online and who they actually are.

BeReal was a symptom. Social media fatigue is the condition.

The question isn't whether people will share their lives online. They will always share.

The question is: **what form of digital identity actually means something now?**

Not who you perform yourself to be. Who you actually are — proven by where you consistently showed up.

The cultural moment Foursquare needed is the cultural moment we're living in.

---

## Two Companies That Validated Our Thesis

They did it separately. We're doing it together.

**Foursquare** proved that check-in behavior works at scale. 55 million users showed that people will voluntarily log presence — repeatedly, without being paid. The behavior is real. Foursquare failed because:
- Check-ins were broadcast in real-time (performance, not memory)
- No institutional anchor — nobody at the Starbucks needed your check-in
- The cultural moment favored posting perfect photos over logging presence

The insight was right. The design and timing were wrong.

**Blackbird** proved that venues pay for identity data. In New York fine dining, restaurants pay monthly SaaS fees to know who their regulars are and recognize them the moment they walk in. Operators will pay real money — not for marketing tools, not for POS software — but specifically for the answer to one question: *who comes back?*

The commercial layer is proven. Blackbird built it for Nobu. We're building it for the places where real communities actually form: campus bars, gyms, startup bootcamps, student organizations.

**Sway is both.**

Check-in behavior that works at scale. Venue operators who pay for the data. Built for the cultural moment that makes the behavior durable. Anchored in the highest-density community environment in human life: the campus ecosystem.

---

## The Mechanism (Why We Persist Where Foursquare Didn't)

Foursquare had no institutional anchor. Nobody at the Starbucks cared if you checked in. The check-in habit lived or died entirely on individual motivation — and individual motivation faded.

Sway's check-in habit is anchored by venue operators with commercial skin in the game.

At Gully's, the operator wants to know who comes back. Their staff has reason to make scanning habitual. The door person says one sentence. The NFC station sits at the entrance chokepoint. The habit is environmentally enforced, not individually motivated.

That institutional anchor is the mechanism Foursquare never had.

Privacy by default is the design that makes it viable for Gen Z. Not "Where am I right now? Tell my friends." Instead: "Where do I consistently show up? Let my life speak for itself."

Different question. Different timing. Different audience. Different design.
