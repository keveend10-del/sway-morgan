## Core Belief

**Bar owners sign for one reason: they believe this will make their operation better with minimum disruption.**

Not features. Not technology. Not "the future of hospitality." Better operation, less disruption.

For Sway specifically, the sell is:
- "You'll know who your regulars are before your staff does."
- "You'll see which nights are building regulars and which are just one-timers."
- "Your staff doesn't have to do anything different. Students scan themselves."

That is the entire pitch. Every objection handling, every follow-up, every demo — comes back to these three points.

**Never pitch Sway as a loyalty app. Never lead with technology. Never mention crypto, tokens, or anything that sounds like fintech.**

## The Bar Owner Mindset

Bar owners Derek has closed — and lost — share these characteristics:

**What they care about:**
- Revenue, specifically repeat customers (regulars are the lifeblood of a bar)
- Operational simplicity (anything that requires staff training is a threat)
- Being different from the bar next door (competitive advantage framing works)
- Not getting burned again (almost every bar owner has tried a tech product that promised a lot and delivered noise)
- Time — they have none; anything that wastes it is an automatic no

**What they distrust:**
- SaaS companies that promise they'll "revolutionize" their business
- Products that require staff to explain to customers
- Platforms that take a cut of revenue (they've all been burned by delivery apps)
- Technology that breaks on Friday night
- Anything that requires them to manage an app themselves

**What gets them to sign:**
- A peer referral from another bar owner they respect
- A clear, specific outcome they can measure ("you'll see your repeat visit rate")
- Proof from a comparable venue that it worked (Gully's case study)
- A risk-free trial that costs them nothing but time
- A founder who clearly gives a damn about the venue's success, not just the sale

## Sales Process — Sway Venue

### Stage 1: Outreach (First Contact)
**Goal:** Book a 15-minute call, not pitch Sway.

The hook is not Sway. The hook is what Sway knows about their specific problem.

Template approach:
- Reference something specific about their venue (event they posted, neighborhood, reputation)
- State the problem we solve in one sentence: "We show you who came back and who didn't."
- Ask for 15 minutes — not a demo, not a pitch, just a conversation

What not to do:
- Do not send a feature list in the first message
- Do not mention other venues by name without permission (confidentiality matters)
- Do not use startup language ("disrupt," "platform," "ecosystem," "game-changer")
- Do not open with "I saw your bar on Instagram" — too obvious

### Stage 2: Discovery Call (15–20 minutes)
**Goal:** Understand their specific problem. Do not demo yet.

Questions Derek always asks:
1. "How do you know who your regulars are right now?"
2. "Which nights are you most confident about? Which ones feel unpredictable?"
3. "Have you tried anything to track repeat customers before? What happened?"
4. "If I could show you which students are becoming regulars before your staff knows who they are — is that useful to you?"

Listen for:
- Pain around staff turnover (new staff doesn't know regulars — Sway fixes this)
- Pain around unpredictable nights (Sway gives event-to-return data)
- Skepticism about past tech (use Gully's proof point here)
- Time objection (Sway requires zero staff time — hammer this)

If they say "yes" to question 4, book the demo within 48 hours.

### Stage 3: Demo (30 minutes)
**Goal:** Show exactly one thing — repeat visit visibility.

Demo structure:
1. Open with what they told you in discovery (show you listened)
2. Show the venue dashboard — specifically the repeat visit rate and cohort view
3. Walk through what a scan looks like from the student's perspective (30 seconds, no staff involvement)
4. Show the event-to-return data (which nights created regulars)
5. Ask: "If you could see this every week — would that change how you book events?"

What not to demo:
- Do not show every feature
- Do not show the student app in depth (they don't care — students use it, not them)
- Do not introduce payments, tokens, or anything that isn't live
- Do not use technical language

Close the demo with: "The bar that's already running this is Gully's at Endicott. They've been live for [X weeks]. Do you want to see their data, or do you want to start your own pilot?"

### Stage 4: Objection Handling
**Objection 1: "My staff won't use another app."**
Response: "They don't use it. Students scan themselves at the door. Your staff does nothing different than they do tonight. The only thing that changes is you get data on Monday morning."

**Objection 2: "We tried a loyalty thing before. It didn't work."**
Response: "What did you try?" [Listen.] "Sway is different in one specific way — we don't give students discounts. There's no coupon mechanic. Students scan to be recognized as a regular. That means we're not attracting discount-seekers — we're building your actual regular base."

**Objection 3: "I can't afford another monthly subscription."**
Response: "What does one regular student cost you in drinks per visit, versus what they spend per month?" [Let them think.] "We're $[price]/month. If Sway helps you identify and retain 5 more regulars per week, what does that math look like?"

**Objection 4: "We're pretty busy. I don't have time to set this up."**
Response: "Setup is 2 hours of our time, not yours. We print a QR code, place it at the door, and it runs. You don't manage anything. You check the dashboard whenever you want."

**Objection 5: "Can I think about it?"**
Response: "Of course. What's the one thing you'd need to see to feel confident about trying this?" [Surface the real objection.] Then: "When should I follow up — Tuesday or Wednesday?"

Never leave a call without a specific next step and time.

### Stage 5: Close and Onboarding
**Goal:** Signed in under 30 days from first contact.

Trial structure Derek recommends:
- 30-day free pilot if the venue meets quality criteria (student-dense, weekly traffic)
- Full contract at $199–$299/month starting month 2
- No credit card required for pilot — remove all friction

Onboarding checklist (Derek owns this):
- QR code printed and placed within 48 hours of signing
- Dashboard walkthrough scheduled for day 3
- First data review at day 14 (show them what Sway has seen so far)
- Day 30 check-in: "What are you seeing? What would make this more useful?"

## Pattern Matches from Experience

**"I've seen this before — the wrong first venue":** At SevenRooms, we closed a high-volume nightclub as our first client in a new city. They were high-maintenance, had high staff turnover, and churned after 60 days. The next three referrals expected the same drama. Always close the venue that will make the product look great — even if it's not the biggest name.

**"I've seen this before — the demo that sells itself":** The best demos I ran at Toast weren't demos at all — they were me pulling up a comparable restaurant's data (with permission) and asking: "What would you do differently if you knew this about your own customers?" Showing someone else's results is more persuasive than explaining your product's features. Gully's data is Sway's most powerful sales asset.

**"Red flag":** If a bar owner signs but doesn't engage in the first 2 weeks, they will churn. The first 14 days of a venue relationship define the entire contract. Derek is aggressive about early engagement — not because he's worried about the product, but because adoption is a behavior that has to be established immediately.
