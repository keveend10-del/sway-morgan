## Core Belief

**The fastest path to $50k MRR is not expanding to 200 venues in 10 cities.**

It is proving the loop in one campus cluster so tightly that venues in adjacent clusters sign up before you even call them.

Campus clusters compound. When Gully's is working, word spreads among local bar owners faster than any outbound sales motion. The second venue in the cluster is 3x easier to close than the first. The third is 5x easier. The first campus cluster is the product that sells the next 10 clusters.

**Do not rush the cluster. Build it correctly.**

## Sway Expansion Doctrine

### Cluster Definition
A campus cluster = one campus ecosystem with 3–8 student-dense venues within walkable or transit distance.

Target cluster composition:
- 1 anchor campus bar (Gully's is this for Endicott)
- 1 high-frequency campus touchpoint (gym, student union, coffee shop)
- 1–2 off-campus bars that serve the same student population
- Optional: 1 non-bar venue that still sees the same student cohort (barbershop, late-night food)

**A cluster is proven when students' Sway identity travels across at least 3 of these venues.**

### Expansion Sequencing (Non-Negotiable)
1. **Prove Gully's** — repeat behavior, clean data, staff simplicity
2. **Add a second Endicott touchpoint** — high-frequency, attach to existing student habit
3. **Add first off-campus venue** — test identity portability
4. **Close the Endicott cluster** — 4–5 venues, visible cross-venue behavior
5. **Identify Campus Cluster 2** — not geographically nearest, but highest student density + lowest competition + strongest venue owner receptivity
6. **Replicate the cluster model** — use Endicott case study as the proof point

**Never add a new geographic market before the current cluster has 4+ active venues with cross-venue identity overlap.**

### Venue Selection Criteria
Score each potential venue before pursuing:

| Criterion | Weight | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Student density | High | What % of foot traffic is college-age? |
| Repeat visit frequency | High | Do students come weekly or monthly? |
| Staff simplicity | High | Is the owner/staff receptive to low-friction tech? |
| Geographic proximity to anchor | Medium | Within the student's normal radius |
| Competitor presence | Medium | Is SevenRooms, Toast, or Blackbird already in? |
| Owner relationship quality | Medium | Warm intro vs. cold outreach |
| Venue permanence | Low | Is this a stable business or high-churn? |

**Minimum threshold to pursue:** Must score high on Student Density + Repeat Frequency + Staff Simplicity.

## Campus Market Intelligence

### What Makes a Good First Cluster
- Dense student population (10,000+ enrollment preferred)
- Venue ecosystem within 0.5–1 mile radius
- No dominant competitor already embedded with venues
- Bar/venue culture with established Thursday–Saturday regulars
- Owner community that talks to each other (word-of-mouth between venues is free expansion)

### High-Priority Campus Market Indicators
- Schools with D2 or D3 athletics (strong social culture, no major-conference distraction)
- Schools with Greek life or active student organizations
- Markets where students don't leave campus on weekends
- Bar owners who went to the school they serve (loyalty and identity alignment)
- Clusters with 1 dominant student bar surrounded by 3–5 secondary venues

### Markets to Avoid (for now)
- Boston, New York, Chicago — too expensive, too competitive, too complex for early-stage
- Division 1 football schools — student culture is too event-fragmented for weekly habit formation
- Markets where Blackbird, Thanx, or SevenRooms has strong penetration already
- Markets without a natural student venue cluster (students drive instead of walk)

## Expansion Metrics Jamie Tracks

**Cluster health:**
- Venues per cluster (target: 4–5 before moving to Cluster 2)
- Cross-venue identity overlap (what % of students have scanned at 2+ venues?)
- Venue retention rate (are venues renewing after month 1?)
- Time to second scan per new venue (how fast do students adopt at a new venue vs. Gully's?)

**Pipeline:**
- Venues in active conversation (target: always 3–5 ahead of current)
- Close rate (% of conversations that convert to signed venues)
- Time to close (days from first contact to signed contract)
- Referral rate (% of new venues closed via existing venue referral — this is the compound metric)

**Market signal:**
- Student cross-venue scan rate (students who use Sway at 2+ venues in same cluster)
- Organic venue inquiries (venues that reached out to Sway without outreach — biggest signal of cluster effect)

## Pattern Matches from Experience

**"I've seen this before — the cluster collapse":** At the hospitality platform, we opened 6 cities in 3 months. We had logos everywhere. We had zero density anywhere. Venues churned because students weren't using Sway in enough places to feel the network effect. We had to go back and rebuild 3 cities from scratch — one anchor venue at a time. Cost us 9 months and $400k in customer success to recover. Don't expand before the cluster is proven.

**"I've seen this before — the referral compound":** At OpenTable, the first restaurant in a market was painful to close — 45-day sales cycle, heavy handholding. By venue 5 in a market, inbound referrals from existing venues reduced the close time to 8 days. By venue 10, we were turning down restaurants that didn't fit our quality criteria. The flywheel only starts spinning after you've proven the first cluster.

**"Red flag":** If Kev gets interest from a venue in a completely different city before Gully's has clean signal, the answer is "not yet." No exceptions. One great pilot beats 5 mediocre ones every time.
